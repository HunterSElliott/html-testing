1Contact Information

Why this matters: Your contact information is the first thing an employer sees. If they cannot reach you or find your work, you do not get the job.

How to do it right:
  • Use a professional email. Your ISU email or firstname.lastname at gmail both work. Do not use a nickname, numbers, or anything unprofessional.
  • Make sure your voicemail greeting is clear and professional.
  • List your city and state so employers know if you are local.
  • If you have a LinkedIn profile, GitHub, or portfolio, include the link. Employers check these before they decide whether to call you.
  • A broken link or missing contact info tells them something about your attention to detail before they read a single bullet point.

2Education

This is the most important section of your resume.

You are in a technical program. Your coursework, your technical skills, and your hands-on lab experience are what employers are paying for. Most of you do not have years of work experience yet. That is okay. What you have is your program.

But listing the course name means nothing on its own.

How to do it right:
  • Select your degree type and program first. The relevant coursework and technical skills will not appear until you do.
  • For every course you select, an expand box will appear. Use it.
  • Write one to two sentences that prove you were in that class and did the work. Not a summary of what the class covered.
  • For technical skills, do the same. Do not just select the skill and leave the expand box empty.
  • Describe a specific situation where you used it.
  • Employers cannot see your lab. You have to paint the picture.

The formula: [Action verb] + [specific task or procedure] + [tool, equipment, or standard used] + [result or outcome]

3Project, Lab Work, Shop Work, Clinical Experience, Practicum, or Field Work

Why this matters: This section is not just for finished capstone projects. Do not sell yourself short.

Anything you built, practiced, performed, or worked through in a lab, clinical, or classroom setting belongs here. If you are still working on it right now, include it. If it was a semester-long lab progression, include it. If it was a clinical rotation, a hands-on practicum, or a real-world simulation, include it.

You can name it whatever fits: Project, Lab Work, Clinical Experience, Practicum, In Progress, Semester Lab, Field Work. The name does not matter. What matters is that you describe what you did and what you learned. One strong description is worth more than five bullet points of generic job duties.

How to do it right:
  • Use a three-part structure: the problem or goal, what you built or did, and the result.
  • Name the specific tools, software, equipment, or standards you used.
  • If you have a link to your work on GitHub, a portfolio, or a shared document, include it.
  • That link is proof. A project without a link is a claim. A project with a link is evidence.


The formula: [What was the goal or problem] + [what you designed, built, or performed] + [tools and skills used] + [result or outcome]

4Professional and Student Organizations

Why this matters: Involvement in professional organizations or student clubs shows you care about your field beyond just getting a grade. It signals initiative, networking ability, and that you are engaged with the industry. Even one organization on your resume puts you ahead of most entry-level candidates who have none.

How to do it right:
  • List the organization name, your role, and the dates.
  • Then add one sentence describing what you actually did or what you got out of it.
  • If you were just a member, say what the organization does and what you participated in.
  • If you held a leadership role, say specifically what you organized, managed, or contributed.
  • Do not exaggerate. Be honest about your role and your contribution.


Examples:

Member: "CompTIA Student Member, 2024-2026. Attended quarterly webinars on cybersecurity trends and industry certifications, applying current topics to coursework in the ITS program."

Leadership role: "ISU Cybersecurity Club, President, 2025-2026. Organized monthly CTF competitions for 20 members and coordinated two guest speaker events with local IT professionals, developing event planning and team leadership skills."

Healthcare program: "ISU Pharmacy Technology Student Association, Member, 2025-2026. Participated in a community health fair providing OTC medication counseling to the public, applying patient communication skills in a real-world setting."

5Certifications and Licenses

Why this matters: Certifications are industry-standard proof that you can do what you say you can do. Employers in technical fields use them as a screening tool: candidates with certifications move to the top of the pile. An entry-level IT job without CompTIA A+, or a pharmacy tech role without PTCB, is significantly harder to land than one with those credentials.

How to do it right:
  • Select your degree type and program in the Education section first. Program-specific certification suggestions will not appear until you do.
  • List the certification name exactly as it appears on your certificate. Not a shortened version or a guess at the name.
  • Include the date you earned it.
  • If you are actively preparing for a certification right now, list it with your expected completion date.
  • Do not list certifications you have not earned and are not currently studying for.
  • If your program includes a certification exam as part of the curriculum, contact your instructor to find out your scheduled exam date and include it here as an expected completion.


Examples:

6Work Experience

Why this matters: Work experience shows you can apply your skills in a real environment with real deadlines, real problems, and real expectations. It proves you have shown up, done the work, and delivered results: and that matters to employers regardless of where you worked. Your job does not have to be in your field. Every job teaches you something worth listing.

How to write a strong bullet point: Every bullet point needs three things: an action verb, a technical skill or tool, and a result or outcome. That is the formula. Start with what you did, add how you did it, and end with what happened because of it. Your technical and soft skills must be woven directly into these bullets: they will not appear anywhere else on your resume.

The formula: [Action verb] + [what you did] + [technical skill or tool used] + [result or outcome]

Action verbs to start with: Managed, Operated, Trained, Resolved, Processed, Maintained, Diagnosed, Implemented, Reduced, Coordinated, Assembled, Calibrated, Inspected, Prepared, Communicated, Organized, Monitored, Performed, Documented, Assisted


How to show soft skills: Do not say "I am a great communicator." Show it. "Trained three new employees on store procedures during their first week" is communication. "Resolved a customer complaint independently without escalating" is problem solving. "Maintained zero cash handling errors over eight months" is attention to detail. Let your actions prove the skill: never just list it.

Examples by job type:

McDonald's Crew Member
  • Prepared food orders during peak lunch rush serving over 100 customers per hour, maintaining accuracy and speed while following food safety protocols.
  • Trained two new crew members on register procedures and kitchen standards during their first week.
Lawn Maintenance and Landscaping Crew
  • Operated commercial mowers, trimmers, and blowers to maintain 15 to 20 residential properties per day following a set route and safety procedures around obstacles and pedestrians, consistently finishing routes on schedule with zero customer complaints over a full season.
Amazon Warehouse Associate
  • Picked and packed over 300 orders per shift using warehouse management software with a 99 percent accuracy rate, consistently meeting daily productivity targets.
  • Identified a recurring labeling error on a product SKU and reported it to the floor manager, preventing an estimated 50 mislabeled shipments per week.
Restaurant Server and Host
  • Managed a section of 6 to 8 tables during dinner rush using the restaurant's point of sale system to enter orders and process payments, maintaining an average customer satisfaction score above 4.5 out of 5.
  • Trained two new hosts on seating rotation and reservation procedures, reducing average wait time complaints during peak hours.

7Awards and Recognition

Why this matters: Awards show that someone noticed your work and thought it was worth acknowledging. Dean's list says you performed under pressure. A scholarship says someone invested in you. Employee of the month says your employer valued what you brought. These details add weight to your resume and separate you from candidates with similar qualifications.

How to do it right:
  • List the award name and the date.
  • Then add one sentence of context: what it took to earn it or what you were doing at the time.
  • That context is what makes an award meaningful.
  • "Dean's List" is three words. "Dean's List, May 2025: Maintained 3.7 GPA while working 25 hours per week and completing CompTIA A+ certification" tells a story about who you are.


Examples:

"Dean's List, December 2025. Achieved 3.8 GPA during first semester of the Pharmacy Technology program while working 20 hours per week at Walgreens."

"ISU College of Technology Excellence Award, April 2026. Recognized by faculty for outstanding performance in the ITS program and contributions to peer learning in lab settings."

"Employee of the Month, September 2025. Recognized for diagnosing and resolving a recurring network connectivity issue that had been open for three weeks without resolution."

Do not be modest. If you earned it, put it in.
